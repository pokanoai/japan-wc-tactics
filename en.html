Tactical Lessons from the 2022 World Cup

vs Germany (2-1 Win) — Lessons

First Half: Japan absorbed pressure in a 4-2-3-1 mid-block. Germany held 74% possession, but Japan kept the center compact. Conceded one goal from a penalty.

Second Half Revolution: Switched to 3-4-2-1, bringing on Doan and Mitoma. The wing-backs' high positioning exploited Germany's high line. Pressing intensity skyrocketed, dismantling Germany's build-up. Doan equalized from a press-triggered chance; Asano's run behind sealed the comeback.

Lesson: In-game system changes can be a devastating weapon.

vs Spain (2-1 Win) — Lessons

First Half: Allowed Spain 80% possession. Formed a 4-4-2/4-5-1 defensive block, restricting passes to peripheral areas. Conceded to Morata but didn't collapse.

Second Half: Again deployed aggressive substitutions and system adjustments. Doan equalized from outside the box; Tanaka scored the winner. Second-half pressing intensity was among the best in the tournament.

Lesson: You can win while conceding 80% possession. Speed in transition is the ultimate game-changer.

Tactical Philosophy

The foundational philosophy underpinning every tactical decision. When every player internalizes these principles, in-game decision-making becomes consistent and cohesive.

Five Core Principles

  • Principle 1: Courage on the Ball Never fear building from the back. Possession is constructed with all 11 players, including the GK. When pressed, stay composed and exploit the space behind the press. Use the opponent's pressing as an invitation to play through them.
  • Principle 2: Immediate Recovery Culture Attempt to win the ball back within 5 seconds of losing it. Gegenpressing is a shared mindset across the entire squad. If recovery isn't possible, rapidly reorganize the defensive shape. Channel Japan's work ethic and endurance into pressing intensity.
  • Principle 3: Positional Fluidity Rather than fixed positioning, players move based on "Relational Play" — defined by dynamic relationships between teammates. Players execute roles, not rigid positions, while maintaining structural integrity — a balance of freedom and order.
  • Principle 4: Half-Space Dominance Dominate the "half-spaces" — the channels between the touchline and the center of the pitch — in both attack and defense. Penetration into these zones generates the highest threat (based on xT data).
  • Principle 5: Game State Adaptation Clearly switch tactics based on the match situation — leading, trailing, or level. Decisions are driven by data and principles, not emotion. Every player understands how to execute in-game formation changes.

3-2. Major Global Tactical Trends

1

The Inverted Full-Back Revolution

Full-backs move into central midfield during build-up play, creating numerical superiority in the middle. Pioneered by Arteta (Arsenal) and Guardiola (City).

Significance for Japan: Tomiyasu's intelligence and versatility make him the ideal player for this role. Build-up stability would improve dramatically.

2

Relational Play

An evolution of Positional Play. Instead of fixed positioning, players move based on dynamic "relationships" — distance, angle, and timing with teammates. Theorized by De Zerbi.

Significance for Japan: Highly compatible with Japanese players' innate sense of cooperation and teamwork. Maximizes organic collective movement.

3

The Evolution of the High Press

Pressing has evolved from brute force to strategic systems using "press triggers" and "press traps." Quantitatively managed through PPDA (Passes Per Defensive Action).

Significance for Japan: Japan's running ability and organized movement can be elevated into a strategic press. Intelligent pressing design overcomes physical disadvantages.

4

The Set-Piece Revolution

Data-driven set-piece design is advancing rapidly. Specialist coaches have become standard. Scientific analysis of near-post, far-post, and penalty-spot areas.

Significance for Japan: The greatest opportunity to compensate for physical disadvantages through intelligence and quality of movement. Over 30% of World Cup goals come from set pieces.

5

The Goalkeeper as the 11th Outfield Player

"Salida Lavolpiana" — GKs actively participate in build-up play. Footwork has become a GK selection criterion. Elite GKs are now expected to achieve 90%+ short-pass accuracy.

Significance for Japan: Adds an extra passing outlet against the opponent's press. Passing ability should be added to the GK selection criteria.

6

"New Verticality"

In the 2025-26 season, the Premier League's average passes per match (713) is the lowest since 2016-17. Long-pass ratio has risen from 10.5% to 11.5%. A shift away from possession-obsession toward "verticality and directness" is underway.

Significance for Japan: Direct transition attacks leveraging the speed of Mitoma and Kubo align perfectly with this global trend. Banish meaningless ball circulation; embrace the courage to play forward.

7

Game State Management

Systematically adjusting formation, pressing intensity, and defensive line height based on match context — leading, trailing, or level.

Significance for Japan: The successful second-half tactical changes in the 2022 World Cup matches against Germany and Spain can now be systematized and institutionalized.

Modern Football Theory

Advances in data science have revolutionized football tactical analysis. Here we explain the theoretical foundations underpinning this plan.

4-1. Data Analytics Framework

Attacking Metrics
  • xG (Expected Goals) — Quantifies shot quality and volume. Calculated from shot location, angle, and match context
  • xT (Expected Threat) — Measures the increase in threat generated by ball movement. Half-space penetration produces the highest xT values
  • Progressive Passes — Passes that advance the ball a significant distance toward the opponent's goal. Evaluates build-up quality
  • Progressive Carries — Distance gained through dribbling. Quantifies individual driving ability of players like Mitoma and Kubo
  • Possession Value (EPV) — Expected scoring value of each possession. Distinguishes between meaningless circulation and high-value possession
  • DxT (Dynamic Expected Threat) — A cutting-edge metric introduced in 2025. Recalculates static xT dynamically using real-time positional data of all players. Validated at Euro 2024
Defensive Metrics
  • PPDA — Passes Per Defensive Action. Lower values indicate higher pressing intensity (Target: below 8.0)
  • Defensive Line Height — Average position of the last defensive line. High-line target: 40m from own goal or higher
  • Counter-Press Success Rate — Rate of immediate ball recovery after losing possession. Target: 35% or above
  • xGA (Expected Goals Against) — Expected goals conceded, calculated from the quality of shots faced
  • Progressive Passes Allowed — Number of forward passes allowed to the opponent. Reflects the density of the defensive structure

4-4. Cutting-Edge Pressing Theory

Press Triggers

Opponent actions that signal the start of pressing:

  • Back Pass — A backward or lateral pass triggers the press
  • Poor First Touch — Press immediately when the opponent miscontrols
  • After Forcing Play Wide — Drive the ball to the touchline, then press collectively
  • Pass Back to GK — Press calibrated to the GK's distribution ability

Press Traps

Intentionally leaving a passing lane open, then intercepting the predictable pass:

  • Cover Shadow — A single player's body position blocks multiple passing lanes
  • False Open Space — Making space appear available while setting a trap
  • Forcing Play Wide — Shutting down the center to funnel the ball to the flanks

4-5. The Set-Piece Revolution

Data: In the 2025-26 Premier League, 19% of all goals have come from corner kicks — an all-time high. Goals from throw-in set pieces average 0.12 per match (roughly one every weekend). Arsenal have scored 32% of their league goals from set pieces. Germany scored 5 of 8 goals from set pieces in World Cup qualifying. This has been called the "NFL-ification of football" — a revolution of rehearsed dead-ball plays.

Approximately 30-35% of World Cup goals come from set pieces. Data-driven set-piece design can become the greatest scoring weapon for Japan, who are physically disadvantaged in the air.

Shape-Shifting Mechanism

Out of Possession (4-2-3-1): Compact 4-2-3-1 defensive block. Both full-backs join the back line to form a back four.

In Possession (3-2-4-1): The left full-back (Tomiyasu) is absorbed into the CB line, creating a back three. The right-back (Sugawara) pushes high. The No. 10 (Kamada) drops into the left half-space.

Role Definitions

  • GK (Suzuki): Origin of build-up. Forms a diamond with the 3 CBs + GK to play through the opponent's press
  • 3 CBs: Wide CBs spread to provide width; the central CB secures the passing lane to the anchor
  • Double Pivot (Endo & Morita): The heartbeat of ball circulation. One drops to receive; the other shows between the lines
  • Mitoma (LW): Hugs the touchline in a high position to isolate for 1v1s. Delivers final balls from the cutback zone
  • Kubo (RW): Drifts into the half-space for creative play. Cuts inside for shooting opportunities
  • Kamada (IH/No. 10): Receives between the lines. Half-turn through balls. Late runs from the third line
  • Ueda (CF): Creates a focal point at the top. Runs in behind to push the defensive line deeper, opening space for teammates to exploit
Key Point: Tomiyasu's inverted full-back role is the cornerstone of this system. His experience at Arsenal can be directly transferred to the national team, making the adaptation cost extremely low.

Defensive Principles

A defensive system that leverages Japan's running power and organizational strength. Overcoming physical disadvantages through "intelligent pressing."

9-3. Counter-Press (Gegenpressing)

Principle: For the first 5 seconds after losing the ball, treat it as an "extension of the attack" and commit fully to winning it back. If not recovered within 5 seconds, immediately reorganize the defensive shape. This switching decision defines the outcome of matches.

Transitions

Attacking-to-defensive and defensive-to-attacking transitions are the phases most directly linked to goals in modern football. Maximizing Japan's speed and rapid switching ability.

Attacking → Defensive Transition (Negative)

Immediate Recovery or Immediate Retreat

  • 5-Second Rule — Counter-press for 5 seconds after losing the ball. 2-3 players near the loss point press immediately
  • Retreat Decision — If not recovered in 5 seconds, use a "foul or deliberate delay" to buy time, then retreat as a unit
  • Rest Defense — Always keep "insurance" against counters during attacks. 2 CBs + anchor always cover the last line
  • Strategic Fouling — Stop dangerous counters with intentional fouls (accept yellow cards if necessary)
Defensive → Attacking Transition (Positive)

Finish Within 10 Seconds

  • First Option — Target the space behind the opponent's last line with the very first pass after winning the ball. Utilize the pace of Mitoma and Kubo
  • Second Option — If no space behind, advance quickly while transitioning into possession
  • CF's Run in Behind — At the moment of ball recovery, Ueda sprints in behind. First action: beat the defensive line
  • Exploit Numerical Advantage — In counter-attacks, quickly create 2v1 or 3v2 situations and finish simply
  • Dribble Counter — If Mitoma receives the ball in open space, he beats the defender 1v1 and drives to the finish

Set-Piece Strategy

Over 30% of World Cup goals come from set pieces. For Japan, this is the ultimate weapon to overcome physical disadvantages. Introducing data-driven set-piece design.

11-1. Attacking Set Pieces

Corner Kicks (Attacking)

  • Short Corners (40%) — Create numerical advantage and change the angle. Draw out defenders
  • Near-Post Flick-On (25%) — Meet the ball at the near post to redirect. Disrupts marker timing
  • Far-Post Zone Attack (20%) — Combine runners to the far post with decoy runs
  • Penalty Spot Area (15%) — Crowded set plays near the penalty spot
  • Takers: Kubo (inswing), Doan (outswing)

Free Kicks (Attacking)

  • Direct FK: Kubo and Doan's left foot — curling over the wall
  • Indirect FK (Near Post): Short pass → cross combination
  • Set Plays: Prepare 5-6 variations. Analyze the opponent's setup and exploit weaknesses
  • Second-Ball Positioning: Place long-range shooters outside the penalty area

Throw-In Tactics

  • Long Throw: Through specialized training, make throw-ins deep in the opponent's half as threatening as corners
  • Short Throw Combinations: Third-man movement to evade the press and retain possession
  • Throw-In Coach: Introduce a specialist throw-in coach, Liverpool-style

11-2. Defensive Set Pieces

Critical Priority: For Japan, who are disadvantaged aerially, defending set pieces is the greatest risk for conceding goals. A hybrid of zonal and man-marking is adopted, with clearly defined responsibilities for each player.
  • Hybrid Defensive System Zonal marking as the base, with man-marking assigned to 2 players at the near post and 1 at the far post. 3-4 players in the central zone defend zonally. Zonal defenders don't just "guard space" — they attack the ball's landing point.
  • Short Corner Defense One player always steps out to contest short corners. Remaining players hold their zones. Build in coverage for the gap left by the stepping-out player.
  • Second-Ball Defense Position 2 players outside the penalty area to collect clearances. They also serve as the starting point for counter-attacks.
